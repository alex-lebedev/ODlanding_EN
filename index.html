Scientific Foundation

Scientific Basis Image

The interdisciplinary foundations of the Open Dialogue Method are rooted in advanced scientific fields, combining principles from the theory of functional systems, network science, neuroscience, evidence-based psychology, and social sciences. This approach allows for a deeper understanding of human interaction with oneself and the world, helping to overcome barriers that hinder goal achievement.

P.K. Anokhin's Theory of Functional Systems: An internationally recognized theory that plays a central role in the Open Dialogue Method. Anokhin demonstrated how complex organisms and social groups develop goal-oriented behaviors using mechanisms of self-organization and dynamic feedback.

Modern Network Science and Neuroscience: In Open Dialogue, complex systems—whether an organism, family, or organization—are viewed as holistic structures whose emergent properties manifest under conditions of flexible and supportive dialogue. It is this flexible interaction, rather than traditional "control," that reveals the natural qualities of these systems and promotes their long-term, sustainable, and positive changes.

Evidence-Based Psychology and Social Sciences: Current research highlights the significant role of social connections in human behavior and overall physical and mental health. Additionally, studies focus on cognitive biases and the potential negative consequences of unhealthy relationships that can impede personal goals. The Open Dialogue environment acts as a "Social Reactor," where each participant gains support in self-understanding, overcomes barriers, and learns to build healthy connections that foster personal growth and goal achievement.
